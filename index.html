The Playbook

When a community's workforce housing shortage gets bad enough, certain institutions become motivated capital partners — hospitals, universities, large regional employers who need workforce to function. The developer who already knows those relationships is in a different position than the one who shows up cold.

At Dvele, I spent three years building that knowledge in Montana. EDO relationships across the state. Anchor institution partnerships assembled before we needed them. When the time came: $2M in soft commitments across TIF, SSBCI, C-PACE, and an infrastructure loan buydown through the Montana Board of Investments.

The money finds the developer that already knows the ecosystem.

The AI Infrastructure

I started building an AI infrastructure thinking the work was the architecture. Then the patterns started showing up — in the technology's behavior and in my own habits as a chief of staff. Over 60 sessions, those two threads produced an operating system that now runs the research.

For non-dilutive capital work: the output is a map of which communities have pre-assembled the infrastructure — EDO programs, employer-assisted housing funds, public land contributions, anchor institution pre-lease commitments. Built in a fraction of the time it takes manually. Walk into any market with that picture already drawn.

The Research

This is not a prescription for ReFrame's roadmap. This is what the playbook produces.

Kalamazoo, MI Bronson Hospital identified the gap, contributed matching funds to unlock a $2M state grant from MSHDA's Employer-Assisted Housing Fund, then contracted Bogan Developments to build 85 units, 20 reserved for hospital employees. The assembled capital came first. The developer came second.
Flagstaff, AZ Mountain Line transit authority, the City of Flagstaff, Coconino County, and Northern Arizona University assembled a 4.3-acre public parcel and issued a formal RFP. Servitas won. Public land contribution, municipal fee reductions, outside developer. Same sequence.
Madison, WI UW-Health, SSM Health, American Family Insurance, and other major regional employers pooled $11M into a revolving loan fund managed by the Madison Development Corporation. Subordinate gap loans at 4.5% fixed, 30-year terms, available to outside private developers. The employers organized the capital. Outside developers deploy against it.
Belgrade, MT Bozeman Health pre-committed to a block of units in ERES Capital's 170-unit development, covering up to 40% of initial demand. One anchor institution pre-lease, non-dilutive, project-enabling.
